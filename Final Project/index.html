Men are affected by mental health too

It is a common misconception that men are less likely to have mental health conditions, like depression. In fact, in 2019, men died of suicide at a rate 3.7 times higher than that of women.

Though biological factors can play a role, the cause for this huge disparaity is gender stereotypes, coping mechanisms, among other assumptions about who experiences mental health conditions.

Why men are less likely to reach out

According to the National Institute of Mental Health, men are less likely to recieve mental health treatment than women. Men can find it difficult to be open about their mental health and seeking help because of cultural stereotypes around how men should act, such as "appearing strong".

Moreover, those who do not speak about their feelings might have difficulty recognizing symptoms of mental health issues.

Noticing mental health symptoms

Mental health symptoms in men include:

  • Anger and agressiveness
  • Irritability
  • Frustration
  • Substance abuse
  • Difficulty focusing
  • Anxiety, feelings of worry
  • High-risk behavior
  • Thoughs of suicide

Sometimes, mental health conditions can have physical symptoms, such as

  • Changes in appetite and mood
  • Aches and Pains
  • Trouble Sleeping
  • Sleeping More than Usual

Oftentimes, loved ones will notice these symptoms first because it is difficult to recognize these problems when you are experiencing them.
